We can now summarize the assumptions made so far:
(the number and order are not important; different sources sometimes combine these statements into fewer, sometimes split them into more numerous ones)

    The first few assumptions are part of a more general kinetic theory of matter:

  1. Matter consists of particles; the total mass of these particle is the mass of the substance/object.
  2. These particles are in constant, random motion, as a result of which they collide among themselves and (in case of a fluid) with the walls of the container.
  3. All these collisions are perfectly elastic.
  4. The average kinetic energy of the particles depends only on the absolute temperature of the system.

    5. [One can argue about whether (4) is an assumption or a deduction]

    The next assumptions are applicable to gases only, and complete the kinetic theory of gases:

    All of the following are negligible (= zero) in a gas:

  5. The total volume of the individual particles compared to the volume occupied by the gas
    (= the average distance separating the gas particles is large compared to their size)
    (= the particles are treated as point masses: they have a mass, but no size);
  6. The interactions among molecules, except during collisions
    (= they exert no long-range (a.k.a. inter-molecular) forces on one another)
    (= between collisions particles travel with uniform velocity);
  7. The duration of individual collisions compared to the time between successive collisions
    (= all changes in momenta, and transfers of kinetic enegy, are instantaneous).
