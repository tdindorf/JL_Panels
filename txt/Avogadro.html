The three gas laws, Boyle-Mariotte (p-V), Charles (V-T), and Gay-Lussac (p-T), allow us to predict the future state of a confined gas, but offer no insight into the underlying mechanism

After analyzing the results of (one of many) Gay-Lussac’s experiments on the chemistry of gases (the law of combining volumes) Avogadro postulated in 1811 that, assuming matter (including gases) consisted of particles,

equal volumes of gases at equal temperatures and pressures must contain equal numbers of particles (Avogadro’s law, or the law of equal volumes)

Not until a century later (1910), when R. Millikan measured the charge of an electron, was it first possible to calculate this number, now known as the Avogadro constant, NA.

We know now that, for any gas at 0 °C (273.15 K) and 1 atm (101.325 × 103 Pa), a volume of 22.414 × 10-3 m3 contains NA = 6.02 × 1023 particles.

What Avogadro appeared to be most interested in, however, were the consequences of his realization: that, unlike in solids or liquids,

the density of a gas was a direct measure of the mass of its individual particles.
Thus the molecular mass of any solid or liquid could be determined by
  1. converting it to a gas (boil, melt and boil, or sublimate), then
  2. comparing the density of the vapor to that of hydrogen (used as the ‘unit’) under the same pressure and temperature,

which allowed for the development of stoichiometry.
